# Project Brief Quality Criteria

What makes a good project brief — evaluated at the appropriate level of maturity. A loose set of notes and a formal structured brief are different artifacts, and the quality bar should match the artifact's stage.

The skill should assess maturity honestly, not penalize early-stage thinking for being early-stage. For rough notes, feedback focuses on what's missing to progress to the next stage. For structured briefs, the bar rises to match a PRD's problem and scope sections.

---

## Calibrating to Maturity

Before applying the criteria, assess the brief's level of structure:

**Loose notes / early exploration** — fragmented, conversational, may lack headers or explicit sections. The question is: is the essential signal here? Problem framing, scope edges, and key unknowns should be surfacing, even informally.

**Working brief** — has sections, but may still be evolving. Criteria apply more fully. Gaps should be named specifically so the PM knows what to add.

**Structured brief** — detailed, nearly PRD-level. Apply full rigor. At this stage, the same standards as a PRD's problem and scope sections apply. If it's structured enough to be mistaken for a PRD, review it as one.

---

## The Criteria

### 1. Problem Framing Is Present

Even rough notes should have a "why this matters" signal. The problem doesn't need to be perfectly bounded, but the reader should understand what the brief is trying to solve and for whom. A brief that jumps straight to the solution without establishing the problem is building on an assumption.

**Evaluate:** Can you restate the problem in one sentence from reading this brief? Is the user or affected segment named? Does the brief explain why this problem matters now?

**For loose notes:** Is there a signal of the problem, even if informal? ("users keep asking for X" counts.)
**For structured briefs:** Apply the same standard as PRD criterion 1 — specific, user-grounded, bounded.

### 2. Scope Signal Exists

Some indication of what this covers and what it doesn't, even if informal. The brief doesn't need a formal in/out-of-scope table, but it should give the reader a working sense of the boundaries. A brief with no scope signal makes it impossible to estimate effort, sequence work, or know when it's done.

**Evaluate:** Would two people reading this independently agree on approximately what's in scope? Is there any signal about what's out of scope, even if implicit?

### 3. Key Decisions Are Surfaced

The choices that will need to be made are named, not assumed. A brief that presents a single path as the obvious answer without acknowledging alternatives has buried its assumptions. Good briefs identify the decisions ahead — what to build, who to build it for, what to trade off — even when those decisions aren't yet made.

**Evaluate:** What are the 2-3 consequential decisions this initiative will require? Are they named in the brief? Are alternatives acknowledged, even briefly?

### 4. Open Questions Are Honest

What's unknown is stated explicitly, not papered over. A brief that looks complete but has implicit unresolved assumptions is more dangerous than one that lists its open questions. Honest open questions signal a PM who understands their own knowledge gaps.

**Evaluate:** Are the genuine unknowns surfaced? Are they listed as open questions, or buried in the narrative? Is anything stated with more confidence than the evidence supports?

### 5. Audience and Purpose Are Clear

Who this brief is for and what action it's meant to drive. A brief written for a stakeholder alignment meeting is different from one written to kick off engineering scoping. If the audience and purpose are implicit, the brief may be calibrated wrong for its context.

**Evaluate:** Is it clear who should read this and what they're expected to do with it? Would the intended reader know what they're being asked to decide or act on?

### 6. For Structured Briefs: Success Is Defined

If the brief is mature enough to be moving toward execution, it should have a success definition. Not necessarily full PRD-level metrics, but a clear answer to: "How will we know this worked?"

**Evaluate:** Is there a success definition? Is it measurable, even roughly? Is it tied to user outcomes rather than delivery milestones?

---

## Using These Criteria

**For review (`doc-review`):** Calibrate to the brief's maturity level, then evaluate against each criterion. Name the maturity level at the top of the review. Prioritize feedback based on what would move the brief to the next stage — don't critique early-stage notes for lacking polish.

**Rating scale:**
- **Strong** — The criterion is clearly satisfied given the brief's stage.
- **Needs work** — Partially addressed but has gaps worth naming.
- **Missing** — Not addressed; the gap will cause problems at the next stage.
- **Not applicable at this stage** — The criterion is appropriate for a more mature version of this brief.
