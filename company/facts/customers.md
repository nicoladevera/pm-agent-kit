# Customer Context

<!-- Populate this file during your first week at a new company. Skills that reference this file produce more specific, credible output when it's filled in — particularly user-feedback, prd-draft, business-case, competitive-intel, and data-analysis. Without it, those skills proceed but flag that customer context is missing. -->

## Buyer vs. User

<!-- Who signs and who uses — this distinction matters most in B2B, but even B2C products have it (e.g., parents buying for kids; IT admins buying for employees).
- Economic buyer: who approves the purchase and what they care about
- End user: who uses the product day-to-day and what they care about
- Where their incentives align and where they diverge
- Who has influence on renewal or cancellation
-->

## Customer Segments

<!-- Who uses the product. For each segment:
- Name / label
- Approximate size (users, accounts, revenue — whatever the company tracks)
- Jobs-to-be-done (what they're trying to accomplish, not what features they use)
- What triggers them to look for a solution
- How they differ from other segments
-->

## Pain Points and Buying Triggers

<!-- The specific pains that drive purchase or adoption.
- What's broken or frustrating before they find this product
- What changes in their world that makes them start evaluating (trigger event)
- What "good enough" looks like to them — the bar for switching
- Pain points by segment if they differ meaningfully
-->

## Key Accounts

<!-- Logos or reference customers worth knowing. Relevant for B2B; for B2C, this is notable cohorts or high-value user profiles.
- Account name (or cohort label)
- Why they matter (size, reference value, strategic fit, design partner status)
- What they use the product for
- Any known risks or sensitivities
-->

## Discovery and Evaluation

<!-- How customers find and assess the product.
- Primary discovery channels (paid, organic, word of mouth, sales-led, product-led)
- What they compare the product against during evaluation
- Common objections that surface before purchase or activation
- What tips them toward committing vs. walking away
-->

## Voice-of-Customer Channels

<!-- Where customer signal surfaces. Knowing the channels helps weight the signal correctly.
- Channel name (e.g., support tickets, NPS verbatims, app store reviews, user interviews, sales call notes, community Slack, social)
- Signal type (complaints, feature requests, churn reasons, praise)
- Volume and cadence (daily / weekly / per-quarter)
- Known biases (e.g., app store reviews skew toward extremes; support tickets skew toward friction)
- Who owns each channel and where to access it
-->

## Churn and Retention Signals

<!-- What predicts customers staying vs. leaving.
- Leading indicators of churn (behavioral signals that precede cancellation or disengagement)
- Leading indicators of retention or expansion (signals that correlate with long-term value)
- Patterns in churned accounts or segments (common reasons, timing, trigger events)
- What re-engages lapsed users or customers, if known
-->
